Decent Work and Economic Growth

Introduction

In a world that has been largely powered by industrial progress and import/export, it makes sense that these processes are also directly linked to economic growth. Over the past 25 years the number of workers living in extreme poverty has declined dramatically, despite the lasting impact of the 2008 economic crisis and global recession. In addition, the middle class now takes up 34% of the population, tripling the number that was present only a few decades ago. This rapid growth is not sustainable, however. We are already seeing slower growth, widening inequalities, and not enough jobs to keep up with a growing labour force. This rapid growth also brings unemployment as different jobs are created, but the same amount, if not more are lost. According to the United Nations, "encouraging entrepreneurship and job creation are key to this". Having people set up their own businesses and creating their own cash flow is key to catalyzing economic growth in these regions. The eighth UNSDG strives to achieve stable economic growth and enough employment by 2030 for men and women alike.

Decent Work and Economic Growth

Explanation

Increasing Wealth

In terms of increasing wealth for all wealth demographics across the board, there are promising results. It has been projected that by 2030, 5.4 billion people will be middle-class, up from 3 billion in 2015 according to Standard Chartered and the Brookings Institution. Most of this increase comes from developing nations such as Southern Asia as well as Africa. It is predicted that Europe and North American wealth classes will not change substantially.

Unemployment

Moreover, the world's unemployment rate was at 5.6 percent in 2017, corresponding to 192.7 million unemployed person, pulled from the UNSDG website. In 2017, we had around 300 million workers living in extreme poverty, and over 470 million jobs will have to be created to prevent the labour market from becoming saturate with all of the new entrants into the workforce by 2030.

Economic Growth

Economic growth is quantified in the form of GDP or gross domestic product, which is an indicator of how well a country's overall economy is growing.In terms of economic development, there's also a focus on the the structural, technological and institutional changes in distribution of product.

Development vs Growth

The main difference is that development often includes a plethora of structural changes such as social or institutional change. This is why less developed economies who usually have relatively low GDP "develop" while the developed economies such as those that have already been established (think USA or Europe) “grow”.
